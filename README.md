## Notable County Profiles

### Montgomery County: Growth Without Moderation

Montgomery County (The Woodlands area, north of Houston) presents a fascinating counterexample to the typical suburban shift pattern. Despite experiencing explosive growth similar to other major suburban counties, it has remained consistently Republican:

**Growth & Political Stability (2000-2024):**
- **Population Growth**: 193% increase in voter turnout (103,886 â†’ 304,241 votes)
- **Political Trend**: R+55.2% (2000) â†’ R+45.9% (2024) - Only 9.3-point Democratic shift
- **Peak Republicanism**: R+61.5% in 2012 (Romney's 80.7% of the vote)

**Timeline:**
- 2000: R+55.2% (Bush 77.6%, Gore 22.4%)
- 2008: R+53.1% (McCain 76.6%, Obama 23.4%)
- 2016: R+53.3% (Trump 76.6%, Clinton 23.4%)
- 2020: R+44.4% (Trump 72.2%, Biden 27.8%) - Smallest margin ever
- 2024: R+45.9% (Trump 73.0%, Harris 27.0%) - Slight Republican rebound

**Comparison to Similar Growth Counties:**
Unlike other fast-growing suburban counties, Montgomery has resisted the blue shift:
- **Williamson County** (Austin suburbs): 231% growth, 39.5-point Democratic swing
- **Collin County** (Dallas suburbs): 193% growth, 38.4-point Democratic swing
- **Fort Bend County** (Houston suburbs): 191% growth, 23.1-point Democratic swing, now D+1.6%
- **Montgomery County**: 193% growth, only 9.3-point Democratic swing, still R+45.9%

**Why the Difference?**
Montgomery County's demographics differ from other growth suburbs - it's attracted more conservative-leaning professionals and retirees to planned communities like The Woodlands, while other suburban counties have seen more diverse, younger, and educated professional migration.

### Starr County: The Ultimate Bellwether Flip

Starr County in South Texas represents the most dramatic political transformation in modern Texas history:

**The Transformation:**
- **2000**: D+54.6% - One of the most Democratic counties in Texas
- **2012**: D+73.8% - Peak Democratic performance (Obama 86.9%)
- **2020**: D+5.1% - Collapse to single digits

**What Happened?**
This 70.6-point swing reflects broader trends in the Rio Grande Valley:
- Economic concerns over energy sector jobs
- Cultural conservatism on social issues
- Trump's direct appeal to Hispanic voters
- Border security concerns resonating locally
- Shift from traditional Democratic machine politics

**Impact:**
Starr County's flip symbolizes the broader South Texas realignment, where historically Democratic Hispanic counties have become competitive or Republican-leaning.

## Historical Context: Understanding Texas's Political Evolution

### Demographic Drivers

Texas's political realignment stems from competing demographic forces:

**Urban/Suburban Growth:**
- Major metro areas (Austin, Dallas, Houston) have attracted educated professionals, younger voters, and diverse populations
- Technology sector growth, particularly in Austin
- Migration from blue states (California, New York, Illinois)

**Hispanic Political Realignment:**
- South Texas Hispanic voters shifting Republican, particularly 2016-2024
- Economic concerns (oil/gas jobs, border economy)
- Cultural conservatism on social issues
- Trump's direct outreach to Hispanic voters

**Rural Solidification:**
- Small towns and rural counties becoming more uniformly Republican
- Economic anxiety over traditional industries
- Cultural issues resonating strongly

### Why These Shifts Matter

**National Implications:**
- Texas's 40 electoral votes make it consequential for presidential elections
- If current urban trends continue, Texas could become competitive statewide by 2028-2032
- However, South Texas realignment has delayed this timeline

**Regional Pattern:**
- Texas mirrors broader Sun Belt trends seen in Georgia, Arizona, and North Carolina
- Urban growth driving Democratic gains
- But Texas's unique Hispanic shift bucks national trends

**Down-Ballot Effects:**
- Statewide Republican margins remain comfortable (R+10-15%)
- But individual counties swinging dramatically affects local races
- Competitive county-level races for sheriff, commissioner, district attorney

## Known Limitations

### Data Coverage Gaps

**Missing Years:**
- 2001, 2003, 2005, 2007, 2009, 2011, 2013, 2015, 2017, 2019, 2021, 2023 (odd-numbered years)
- These years had only local/special elections, no statewide races

**Race Selection:**
- Focus on top-of-ticket races (President, Senate, Governor, statewide offices)
- County-level races (Sheriff, Commissioner, DA) not included
- State legislative races not included (would require district mapping)

### Data Quality Notes

**Early Years (2000-2010):**
- OpenElections data quality varies by county
- Some small counties may have minor discrepancies with official canvass
- Independent/third-party candidates sometimes aggregated as "OTHER"

**2020-2024:**
- VTD-level data requires aggregation, possible minor rounding differences
- 2022 supplemental races added from separate file (Land Comm, Ag Comm, RR Comm)

**Third-Party Candidates:**
- Generally aggregated as "other_votes" except 2006 Governor (Strayhorn, Friedman)
- Libertarian candidates excluded from two-party margin calculations

### Technical Limitations

**Browser Compatibility:**
- Requires modern browser with WebGL support for Mapbox GL
- Mobile experience may vary

**Performance:**
- Large JSON file (17+ MB) requires initial load time
- All data loaded at once (no lazy loading)
